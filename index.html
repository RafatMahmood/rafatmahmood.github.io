Rafat Mahmood

I am a Research Fellow at the Department of Economics and the ARC Centre of Excellence for the Elimination of Violence against Women (CEVAW) at Monash University. Previously, I was a Postdoctoral Associate in Economics at New York University Abu Dhabi.

My research spans development economics, political economy, and international security — with a focus on terrorism, gender-based violence, conflict, and identity. I am passionate about using innovative data and empirical methods to understand the forces that shape human welfare and political order.

I completed my PhD in Economics at the University of Western Australia.

Publications

Money from Afar: Analyzing the Link Between Remittances and Terrorism
Forthcoming · European Economic Review
Working Paper

Despite the popular rhetoric around remittances and money transfers from abroad being a convenient source of terrorism financing and the resulting imposition of financial regulations on such service providers, the evidence linking remittances to terrorism is largely insufficient. This study empirically analyzes the relationship between remittances and terrorism using global data from 1970 to 2019. To isolate causality, the study exploits variation in remittances induced by natural disasters in related countries — the relatedness being defined by the share of migrants. The empirical evidence shows a positive relationship between remittances and terrorism that consistently emerges across various specifications, contributing to our understanding of the role of remittances in terrorism.

Gone with the Wind: The Consequences of US Drone Strikes in Pakistan
Economic Journal · 2023
with Michael Jetter
Published Version IZA Working Paper Blog

This paper explores the consequences of the 420 US drone strikes in Pakistan from 2006 to 2016. Our identification strategy exploits wind conditions that complicate the feasibility of drone strikes. We find drone strikes encourage terrorism, causing 17.5 percent of all terror attacks in Pakistan and up to 6,000 terror deaths. Distinguishing between reactions of outsiders (the Pakistani populace) and insiders (terrorists), we analyze the sentiment of US-related coverage in a leading Pakistani newspaper, anti-US protests, and Google searches indicative of radicalization. Our findings suggest outsiders turn against the US and sympathize with insiders because of drone strikes.

Income and Terrorism: Insights from Subnational Data
Journal of Conflict Resolution · 2024
with Michael Jetter and David Stadelmann
Published Version IZA Working Paper

We construct a panel database to study terrorism for 1,527 subnational regions in 75 countries between 1970 and 2014. Results consistently imply an inverted U-shape: the threat of terrorism rises as low-income polities become richer, peaking at GDP/capita levels of approximately US $12,800 (2005 PPP), before falling consistently above that level. This pattern holds for domestic and transnational terrorism alike, and across ideology-specific subsamples. Alleviating poverty may first exacerbate terrorism, contrary to much of the proposed policy recipes since 9/11.

Post-Cold War Civil Conflict and the Role of History and Religion: A Stochastic Search Variable Selection Approach
Economic Modelling · 2022
with Michael Jetter, Christopher F. Parmeter, and Andrés Ramírez Hassan
Published Version IZA Working Paper

Considering 34 potential determinants in 175 post-Cold-War countries, we employ stochastic search variable selection (SSVS) to sort through all 234 possible models to address model uncertainty. We find past conflict constitutes the most powerful predictor of current conflict: path dependency matters. Larger shares of Jewish, Muslim, or Christian citizens are associated with increased conflict, while economic and political factors remain less relevant than colonial origin and religion.

Communications Technology and Terrorism
Journal of Conflict Resolution · 2019
with Michael Jetter
Published Version CESifo Working Paper

Communications technology (CT) can help terrorists spread their message, recruit followers, and coordinate — but it also facilitates monitoring and arrest. Using data for 199 countries from 1970–2014, we find evidence of an inverted U-shape: terrorism peaks at intermediate ranges of CT and corresponding magnitudes are sizeable. Results are robust to a battery of alternative specifications and placebo regressions.

Women in Power: Parliamentary Action, Social Attitudes, and Gender-Based Crime
with Quynh Do, Astghik Mavisakalyan, and Leigh Tyers

Using a regression discontinuity design based on close mixed-gender electoral contests in Australia (2010–2022), we find that female MPs devote significantly more attention to gender-related issues in parliamentary speech, are more likely to support gender-related legislation, and that their electorates experience a meaningful decline in gendered crime rates during the subsequent term. Female representation can shape policy priorities, social attitudes, and downstream gendered outcomes, even within a disciplined party system.

Identity, Poverty and Extremist Attitudes
with Samreen Malik and Jocelyn Belanger
SSRN

Using a novel lab-in-the-field experiment with Madrassa students in Pakistan, we causally examine the impact of Madrassa identity on extremist attitudes across economic strata. Madrassa identity exacerbates extremism among poorer students but mitigates it among wealthier ones, driving both groups toward collective norms. The findings are distinct from religious identity and challenge simplified narratives linking Madrassas to extremism.

Echoes of Violence: Evidence from the Malala Attack and Public Attitudes Toward Women
First Draft

Using nationally representative survey interviews from the World Values Survey conducted in Pakistan, I exploit the precise timing of the 2012 attack on Malala Yousafzai to assess changes in attitudes toward women's rights and education. The analysis provides evidence of regressive shifts in gender-related beliefs, highlighting how a high-profile violent event can influence public discourse on gender equality in patriarchal and conflict-affected settings.

On the Madrassa Conundrum in Terrorism Studies
Drawing on Akerlof and Kranton's concept of identity choice, this paper hypothesizes that madrassa narratives lower the net costs of identity manifestation for potential terrorists through two channels: direct exploitation of madrassa networks for recruitment and fundraising, and indirect shifts in prescriptions of ideal behavior that make it harder to identify and resist extremist tendencies.
